My daily driver is a Dell Latitude 5501 I pulled out of an e-waste bin at a place I used to work. It has an i7-9850H, a GeForce MX150, and a 15.6" 1080p IPS display. It originally came with 8GB of RAM and one 256GB NVMe drive. Very normal business laptop, which is exactly why it's good.

One day a business dropped off about seven of these, all slightly different. Two coworkers grabbed one each. I took the rest. I gave one to my roommate's brother, kept the best one for myself, and sold the other three. Not bad for stuff that was headed to recycling.

I upgraded it to 32GB of RAM and filled every storage slot: two M.2 NVMe SSDs and a 2.5" SATA SSD in the drive bay. The second M.2 slot is supposed to take a short drive. Mine was full-size. I shoved it in there and forced the back panel on. It still works.

Each drive has its own operating system:

- **macOS Tahoe** on one M.2, installed via OpenCore
- **Fedora 43 KDE** on the other M.2
- **Windows 11** on the 2.5" SATA SSD

Three operating systems, three drives, one laptop that someone threw away.

Fedora 43 KDE is the one I use most. It's my main setup for development and general use. I like KDE a lot now. It looks good, gets out of my way, and is easy to tune. I also set up a gaming VM with [Nobara KDE](/blog/nobara-gaming-vm) recently.

macOS is there because I like using it, I need it for cross-platform web testing, and Hackintoshing is fun. Getting macOS running on random PC hardware still feels wrong in a good way. It isn't 100% finished though. Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, USB, speakers, webcam, mic, touchpad, and keyboard all work. External display out still doesn't. And on newer macOS versions the MX150 might as well not exist because Nvidia support is dead.

Windows is there for when I have to do something Windows-only. I barely use it. Every time I boot it, it seems like it wants to update for 10 minutes before I can do anything. I still hate that.

The best part of putting each OS on its own physical drive is that they stay out of each other's way. No bootloader nonsense, no partition juggling, no weird accidents. I just pick a drive from the boot menu and move on.

For a laptop that came out of a bin, this thing is stupidly capable. 32GB of RAM, three SSDs, an i7-9850H, and it handles coding, VMs, and a stupid number of browser tabs without a problem. All it really needed was upgrades and someone willing to tinker with it.

If you're into this kind of stuff, keep an eye on e-waste bins, surplus sales, and business liquidations. There is way better hardware in those places than most people think.
